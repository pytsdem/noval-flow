Definition:
- A content block is a content unit with one writing purpose, not one physical paragraph.
- One block may contain 1 to 3 natural paragraphs.
- Each natural paragraph should remain comfortable for front-end reading.

Core shift:
- Do not only split the chapter brief into information chunks.
- Design each block around how it will make the characters feel more human, more pressured, more costly, and more memorable.
- Think in terms of scene design before drafting prose.

Requirements:
1. Plan 3 to 6 blocks only.
2. Each block should do one main job: opening pressure, relationship collision, clue movement, emotional reprice, ending pull, and so on.
3. Adjacent blocks must clearly change the situation, the cost, the relationship angle, or the reader's emotional state.
4. Respect hidden truths, allowed clues, forbidden reveals, and reader belief.
5. Keep `text` as an empty string, `status` as `draft`, and `version` as `1`.
6. Use stable ids like `ch_002.sc_001.b001`.
7. Every block must include all of these fields:
   - `block_id`
   - `chapter_id`
   - `block_index`
   - `purpose`
   - `characters`
   - `active_lines`
   - `active_twists`
   - `scene_goal`
   - `must_reveal`
   - `must_hide`
   - `emotional_tone`
   - `end_state`
   - `human_reaction_target`
   - `cost_shift`
   - `reader_feeling_target`
   - `paragraph_budget`
   - `style_risk_guard`
   - `text`
   - `status`
   - `version`

Field guidance:
- `human_reaction_target`:
  Write concrete living-human reactions, not abstract plot function.
  Examples: bodily leakage, practical calculation, self-mockery, startled witness reaction, small failure of composure.
- `cost_shift`:
  At the end of the block, someone must pay a little more.
  The cost may be social, bodily, emotional, financial, political, or relational.
- `reader_feeling_target`:
  Say what the reader should directly feel when the block ends.
- `paragraph_budget`:
  Usually 2-4 natural paragraphs, each around 30-120 Chinese characters, with 180 as a danger line.
- `style_risk_guard`:
  Name the two to four easiest ways this block could be written badly.
  Examples: repeating one cold/blood image, summary psychology instead of scene, dialogue that only explains plot.

Planning style:
1. A good block plan is not generic.
2. Prefer pain, price, friction, residue, and relationship re-pricing over flat information delivery.
3. If a clue appears, design the human reaction around it instead of explaining it.
4. If the block is quiet, give the quiet a concrete unease or social pressure.
5. If the chapter ends on a hook, land that hook through a person's reaction or changed situation, not a trailer sentence.
