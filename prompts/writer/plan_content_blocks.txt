Definition:
- A content block is a writing-design unit, not one physical paragraph.
- One block should usually contain 2 to 4 natural paragraphs.
- Plan blocks as if you are a novelist designing scene pressure before writing the prose.

Core shift:
- Do not flatten the chapter brief into information chunks.
- Design each block around human reaction, cost, relationship pressure, and reader feeling.
- A good block plan should already make the later prose harder to write flatly.

Requirements:
1. Plan 3 to 6 blocks only.
2. Each block should do one main job: opening pressure, relationship collision, clue movement, emotional reprice, ending pull, and so on.
3. Adjacent blocks must clearly change the situation, the cost, the relationship angle, or the reader's emotional state.
4. Respect hidden truths, allowed clues, forbidden reveals, and reader belief.
5. Keep `text` as an empty string, `status` as `draft`, and `version` as `1`.
6. Use stable ids like `ch_002.sc_001.b001`.
7. Every block must include these fields:
   - `block_id`
   - `chapter_id`
   - `block_index`
   - `purpose`
   - `characters`
   - `active_lines`
   - `active_twists`
   - `scene_goal`
   - `must_reveal`
   - `must_hide`
   - `emotional_tone`
   - `end_state`
   - `human_reaction_target`
   - `cost_shift`
   - `reader_feeling_target`
   - `paragraph_budget`
   - `style_risk_guard`
   - `text`
   - `status`
   - `version`
8. Add `character_reentry_mode` only when an important already-established character is re-entering the page.
9. Add `clue_reveal_mechanism` only when this block is responsible for naturally exposing a clue.

Field guidance:
- `human_reaction_target`:
  Write concrete living-human reactions, not abstract plot function.
  Good targets include bodily leakage, practical calculation, self-mockery, startled witness reaction, social embarrassment, or a small failure of composure.
- `cost_shift`:
  At the end of the block, someone must pay a little more.
  The cost may be bodily, social, emotional, financial, political, or relational.
- `reader_feeling_target`:
  Say what the reader should directly feel when the block ends, not only what the reader should know.
- `paragraph_budget`:
  Usually 2-4 natural paragraphs, each around 30-120 Chinese characters. Treat 180 Chinese characters as a danger line.
- `style_risk_guard`:
  Name the two to four easiest ways this block could be written badly.
  Examples: repeating one cold/blood image, summary psychology instead of scene, dialogue that only explains plot, turning a person into a puzzle device.
- `character_reentry_mode`:
  Use this only for re-entry, not first introduction.
  Prefer recognition signals such as familiar subordinates, objects, symbols, power arrangements, tone of voice, or one high-value line.
  The returning character's first line or reaction should reveal what they currently care about most.
- `clue_reveal_mechanism`:
  Do not treat clue reveal as author handout.
  Prefer this chain:
  relationship pressure -> avoidance -> bodily slip or object accident -> another person notices -> the clue becomes visible.
  After the clue appears, do not explain its full meaning immediately.

Planning style:
1. Prefer pain, price, friction, residue, and relationship re-pricing over flat information delivery.
2. Each block should make at least one person harder pressed by the end.
3. If a clue appears, design the scene pressure and the refusal to explain around it.
4. If the block is quiet, give the quiet a concrete unease, etiquette pressure, money pressure, bodily strain, or practical concern.
5. If the chapter ends on a hook, land that hook through a changed situation or a person's reaction, not a trailer sentence.
